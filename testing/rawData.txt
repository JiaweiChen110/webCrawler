           <p><b><a href="/wiki/Planet_Nine" title="Planet Nine">Planet Nine</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_hypothetical_Solar_System_objects" title="List of hypothetical Solar System objects">hypothetical</a> <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System#Trans-Neptunian_region" title="Solar System">outer region of the Solar System</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravitational</a> effects could explain the unlikely clustering of <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbits</a> for a group of <a href="/wiki/Extreme_trans-Neptunian_object" title="Extreme trans-Neptunian object">extreme trans-Neptunian objects</a>, bodies with average distances from the Sun that are more than 250 times that of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. These objects tend to make their closest approaches to the Sun in one sector, and their orbits are similarly tilted. Their improbable alignments suggest that an undiscovered planet may be shepherding the orbits of the most distant known <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> objects. Planet Nine would have a predicted mass five to ten times that of Earth, and an <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">elongated orbit</a> extending 400 to 800 times as far from the Sun as the Earth's. It may have been ejected from its original orbit by <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Nebular_hypothesis" title="Nebular hypothesis">genesis</a> of the Solar System, wrested from another <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a>, captured as a <a href="/wiki/Rogue_planet" title="Rogue planet">rogue planet</a>, or pulled into an eccentric orbit by a passing star. (<a href="/wiki/Planet_Nine" title="Planet Nine"><b>Full&nbsp;article...</b></a>) </p>

            <td style="padding:0 6px 0 0"> <p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Oath_of_the_Horatii" title="Oath of the Horatii">Oath of the Horatii</a></b></i> is a large painting by the French artist <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Louis_David" title="Jacques-Louis David">Jacques-Louis David</a> painted in 1784. It depicts a scene from a Roman legend about a seventh-century BC dispute between two warring cities, Rome and <a href="/wiki/Alba_Longa" title="Alba Longa">Alba Longa</a>. Instead of the two cities sending their armies to war, they agree to choose three men from each city; the victor in that fight will be the victorious city. From Rome, three brothers from a Roman family, the <a href="/wiki/Horatii" title="Horatii">Horatii</a>, agree to end the war by fighting three brothers from a family of Alba Longa, the Curiatii. The three brothers, all of whom appear willing to sacrifice their lives for the good of Rome, are shown saluting their father, who holds their swords out for them. The principal sources for the story behind David's <i>Oath</i> are the first book of <a href="/wiki/Livy" title="Livy">Livy</a>, as elaborated by <a href="/wiki/Dionysius_of_Halicarnassus" title="Dionysius of Halicarnassus">Dionysius of Halicarnassus</a>. However, the moment depicted in David's painting is his own invention. The painting led to the popularization of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_salute" title="Roman salute">Roman salute</a> and also increased David's fame, allowing him to take on his own students. It is now in the <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a> in Paris. </p> <p><small>Painting: <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Louis_David" title="Jacques-Louis David">Jacques-Louis David</a></small></p>
